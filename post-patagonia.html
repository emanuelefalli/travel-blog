Among the Quiet Peaks of Patagonia

Torres del Paine, Chile March 2026

We arrived in Puerto Natales on a grey afternoon, the kind of weather that makes you doubt every decision that brought you there. By morning, the clouds had lifted enough to reveal the first ridgeline — and any thought of turning back vanished.

The plan had been simple: eight days on the W trek, four extra to wander. What I had not planned for was the wind. It comes off the Southern Patagonian Ice Field with a kind of patience, as if it has been blowing for ten thousand years and intends to blow for ten thousand more. You learn to lean into it. You learn that your tent will not stand still and that this is fine.

Days of walking, evenings of nothing

There is a rhythm that takes hold on a long trek. You wake before the sun, eat oats with cold water, walk for six or seven hours, set up camp, and watch the light. That is the entire day. It sounds boring written down. It is not.

I took fewer photographs than I expected. The temptation, in a place like this, is to shoot everything — every ridge, every shadow, every patch of lichen on stone. But the mountains do not benefit from being collected. They benefit from being watched.

The pass

On the sixth morning we crossed the John Gardner Pass. The guidebook describes it as the hardest day of the circuit, and the guidebook is correct. What it does not say is how the wind tries to push you backwards down the scree, or how the first sight of the Grey Glacier — vast, blue, impossibly still from above — undoes everything you thought you understood about scale.

I sat on a rock for a long time. I did not take a photograph. I felt very small and very lucky, in that order.

Coming down

We finished on a Sunday, walked into the refugio at Paine Grande, and ordered everything on the menu. The boat across the lake felt unreal — sitting still, being moved, watching the towers shrink behind us. I had been walking for so many days that I had forgotten what it felt like not to.

Back in Puerto Natales the wind was still blowing. I sat in a café with a coffee and a notebook and wrote nothing for a long time.
